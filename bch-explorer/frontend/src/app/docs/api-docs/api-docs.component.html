A mempool (short for "memory pool") is the queue of pending and unconfirmed transactions for a cryptocurrency network node. There is no one global mempool: every node on the network maintains its own mempool, so different nodes may hold different transactions in their mempools.

A mempool explorer is a tool that enables you to view real-time and historical information about a node's mempool, visualize its transactions, and search and view those transactions.

Projected blocks are on the left of the dotted white line, and confirmed blocks are on the right.

A blockchain is a distributed ledger that records the transactions for a cryptocurrency network. Miners amend the blockchain ledger by mining new blocks.

A block explorer is a tool that enables you to explore real-time and historical information about the blockchain of a cryptocurrency. This includes data related to blocks, transactions, addresses, and more.

Mining is the process by which unconfirmed transactions in a mempool are confirmed into a block on a blockchain. Miners select unconfirmed transactions from their mempools and arrange them into a block such that they solve a particular math problem.

The first miner on the network to find a suitable block earns all the transaction fees from the transactions in that block. As a result, miners tend to prioritize transactions with higher transaction fees.

Mining pools are groups of miners that combine their computational power in order to increase the probability of finding new blocks.

Bytes (B) are used to measure the size of transactions and blocks on the Bitcoin Cash network.

A Bitcoin Cash transaction's size in the blockchain is not determined how much Bitcoin Cash it transfers—instead, a transaction's size is determined by technical factors such as how many inputs and outputs it has, how many signatures it has, and the format it uses (normal, etc). Since space in the Bitcoin Cash blockchain is limited, bigger transactions pay more in mining fees than smaller transactions.

Block sizes have a minimum size of 32MB. But the block size can be larger than that, and is calculated dynamically by ABLA (Adaptive Blocksize Limit Algorithm). With ABLA there is no upper limit on the block size (although capped to ~2x increase per year).

The priority of a pending Bitcoin Cash transaction is determined by its feerate. Feerates are measured in sat/B.

Using a higher sat/B feerate for a Bitcoin Cash transaction will generally result in quicker confirmation than using a lower feerate. But feerates change all the time, so it's important to check suggested feerates right before making a transaction to avoid it from getting stuck. That being said, in Bitcoin Cash the fees are low, so you can usually get away with a lower feerate.

There are feerate estimates on the top of the main dashboard you can use as a guide. See this FAQ for more on picking the right feerate.

If it's been a while and your transaction hasn't confirmed, your transaction is probably using a lower feerate relative to other transactions currently in the mempool. Depending on how you made your transaction, there may be ways to accelerate the process.

There's no need to panic—a Bitcoin Cash transaction will always either confirm completely (or not at all) at some point. As long as you have your transaction's ID, you can always see where your funds are.

To get your transaction confirmed quicker, you can set a higher feerate.

Due to its increased block capacity, Bitcoin Cash maintains high throughput during periods of peak demand. Meaning your transaction will most likely end-up in the next block.

Furthermore, Bitcoin Cash has zero confirmation (0-conf). This means that transactions are immediately available for spending, even before they are confirmed in a block. And allow for near-instant peer to peer payments by merchants or for e-commerce. Small transactions are safe to spend immediately without waiting for confirmation.

Bitcoin Cash has big blocks with enough space to handle a lot of transactions even during high traffic. So, your transaction will most likely confirm within the next block and will never get stuck in the mempool.

Here is an overview of BCH Exploer's feerate suggestions:

  • High Priority. This figure is the median feerate of transactions in the first mempool block. Consider using this feerate if you want confirmation as soon as possible.
  • Medium Priority. This figure is the average of the median feerate of the first mempool block and the median feerate of the second mempool block.
  • Low Priority. This figure is the average of the Medium Priority feerate and the median feerate of the third mempool block. Consider using this feerate if you want confirmation soon, and your transaction will already very likely be mined in the next block. On small amount transactions you can even trust on 0-conf.
  • No Priority. This figure is either 2x the minimum feerate, or the Low Priority feerate (whichever is lower). You are still very likl to your transaction confirmed in the next block.

In all cases, the suggested feerate is adjusted lower if any of the mempool blocks involved in the calculation are not full (example: if there is only 1 mempool block that's less than half-full, BCH Explorer will suggest a feerate of 1 sat/B—not the median feerate of transactions in the block).

Mempool blocks use feerates, transaction sizes, and other metrics to forecast which transactions will be in future blocks. Actual blocks will turn out to be different: miners have their own views of the mempool, their own algorithms for determining which transactions to include in a block, etc.

Ultimately, the Bitcoin Cash network is not perfectly predictable, so fee estimation cannot be perfectly precise.

Use BCH Explorer's feerate suggestions as a guide, and understand that they do not guarantee transaction confirmation in any period of time. However, in Bitcoin Cash the fees are low and the block sizes are big enough that most transations are confirmed within the next block.

See the graphs page for aggregate trends over time: mempool size over time and incoming transaction velocity over time.

When a Bitcoin Cash transaction is made, it is stored in a Bitcoin Cash node's mempool before it is confirmed into a block. When the rate of incoming transactions exceeds the rate transactions are confirmed, the mempool grows in size.

By default, Bitcoin Cash Node allocates 320MB of memory for its mempool, so when a node's mempool grows big enough to use all 320MB of allocated memory, we say it's "full".

Once a node's mempool is using all of its allocated memory, it will start rejecting new transactions below a certain feerate threshold—so when this is the case, be extra sure to set a feerate that (at a minimum) exceeds that threshold. The current threshold feerate (and memory usage) are displayed right on BCH Explorer's front page.

When a new block is found, mining pools often send miners new block templates prior to fully validating the new block, often before they've even received the new block. During this time, it is not possible to select transactions for the next block as a pool isn't sure which transactions conflict with transactions already mined.

While empty blocks do not add additional transactions to the blockchain, they do contribute to the overall security of transactions already in the chain.

CashTokens are efficient, native layer-1 tokens built directly into the Bitcoin Cash network. These are digital assets that can be created, owned, and utilized by individuals, organizations, or decentralized applications on the decentralized Bitcoin Cash network. CashTokens are optional to use in the Bitcoin Cash network.

Types of CashTokens:

  • Fungible Tokens: These are traditional tokens where each unit is identical and interchangeable. They can be merged, divided, and used for assets like stablecoins, loyalty points, voting shares, utility tokens, and collateralized loans.
  • Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs): Unique tokens that cannot be merged or divided. In CashTokens, these are authenticated messages that allow contracts to attest to commitments in an impersonation-proof way, enabling advanced covenant functionality.

Use Cases:

CashTokens enable developers to build on-chain decentralized applications including crowdfunding systems, trading protocols, decentralized exchanges (DEXs), cross-chain bridges, and more. They provide the building blocks for complex coordination tasks like liquidity-pooling, auctions, voting, and sidechain operations.

Learn More: Visit cashtokens.org for detailed documentation and technical specifications.

P2S (Pay to Script) is a new output type introduced in Bitcoin Cash's May 2026 network upgrade. It allows the full locking script to be embedded directly in the output; without a hash wrapper, up to 201 bytes in length.

How it differs from P2SH:

  • No hash indirection. P2SH outputs lock funds to a hash of a redeem script; the actual script is only revealed at spend time. P2S outputs contain the locking script directly, making the spending conditions immediately visible on-chain.
  • No payable CashAddress. Because P2S outputs don't follow the standard address format, wallets won't generate a CashAddress for them. This makes P2S safer for contract use. Users can't accidentally send funds to a contract output that may be unrecoverable.
  • More byte-efficient. By skipping the hash/verify overhead of P2SH, P2S reduces the size of many covenant and multi-party contract outputs by around 34 bytes per output.

Use cases: P2S is primarily aimed at developers building vaults, multi-party covenants, decentralized exchanges, and other on-chain applications where the locking conditions should be transparent and directly inspectable by the contract itself.

Learn more: See the CHIP-2024-12 P2S specification for full technical details.

Zero-confirmation (0-conf) transactions are Bitcoin Cash transactions that are processed and confirmed almost instantly, without waiting for the block confirmation process. This allows for near-instant payment processing like in-store purchases or online transactions where speed is important.

Bitcoin Cash has no RBF, CPFP or accelerators, meaning zero confirmations are safe enough for small amounts. While small BCH amounts are safe for 0-conf, we advise on multiple confirmations for (very) large BCH amounts.

ABLA stands for Adaptive Blocksize Limit Algorithm, which is the mechanism that Bitcoin Cash uses to automatically adjust the maximum block size limit based on network demand.

Bitcoin Cash implemented ABLA to allow the block size to grow organically as the network scales. The algorithm monitors block sizes over time and gradually increases the limit when blocks are consistently full, ensuring the network can handle increased transaction volume without requiring hard forks for size adjustments.

The algorithm works by:

  • Tracking the average block size over a recent period
  • Gradually increasing the limit when blocks are consistently near capacity
  • Providing predictable growth patterns for developers and users
  • Ensuring the network can scale to meet growing demand

This approach allows Bitcoin Cash to maintain low fees and reliable transaction confirmation times even as adoption grows, making it suitable for global payment applications.

bchexplorer.cash uses multiple Bitcoin Cash nodes to obtain data: some with the default 320MB mempool memory limit (call these Small Nodes) and others with a much larger mempool memory limit (call these Big Nodes).

Many nodes on the Bitcoin Cash network are configured to run with the default 320MB mempool memory setting. When all 320MB of memory are used up, such nodes will reject transactions below a certain threshold feerate. Running Small Nodes allows bchexplorer.cash to tell you what this threshold feerate is—this is the "Purging" feerate that shows on the front page when mempools are full, which you can use to be reasonably sure that your transaction will be widely propagated.

Big Node mempools are so big that they don't need to reject (or purge) transactions. Such nodes allow for bchexplorer.cash to provide you with information on any pending transaction it has received—no matter how congested the mempool is, and no matter how low-feerate or low-priority the transaction is.

Memory usage on the front page refers to the real-time amount of system memory used by a Bitcoin Cash node's mempool. This memory usage number is always higher than the total size of all pending transactions in the mempool due to indexes, pointers, and other overhead used by Bitcoin Cash Node for storage and processing.

bchexplorer.cash shows the memory usage of a Bitcoin Cash node that has a very high mempool memory limit. As a result, when mempools fill up, you may notice memory usage on bchexplorer.cash go beyond 320MB. This is not a mistake—this memory usage figure is high because it's for a Bitcoin Cash node that isn't rejecting (or evicting) transactions. Consider it to be another data point to give you an idea of how congested the mempool is relative to the default memory limit of 320MB.

A Bitcoin Cash node running the default 320MB mempool memory limit, like most Raspberry Pi nodes, will never go past 320MB of memory usage.

Block validation rules do not strictly require that a block's timestamp be more recent than the timestamp of the block preceding it. Without a central authority, it's impossible to know what the exact correct time is. Instead, the Bitcoin Cash protocol requires that a block's timestamp meet certain requirements. One of those requirements is that a block's timestamp cannot be older than the median timestamp of the 12 blocks that came before it. See more details here.

As a result, timestamps are only accurate to within an hour or so, which sometimes results in blocks with timestamps that appear out of order.

BCH Explorer aims to show you the effective feerate range for blocks—how much would you actually need to pay to get a transaction included in a block.

A transaction's effective feerate is not always the same as the feerate explicitly set for it. For example, if you see a 1 s/b transaction in a block with a displayed feerate range of 5 s/b to 72 s/b, chances are that 1 s/b transaction had a high-feerate child transaction that boosted its effective feerate to 5 s/b or higher. In such a case, it would be misleading to use 1 s/b as the lower bound of the block's feerate range since it actually required more than 1 s/b to confirm that transaction in that block.

You can find a transaction's feerate on its transaction details page.

A block audit visually compares BCH Explorer's expected block to the actual block for a particular block height.

How is the expected block determined? BCH Explorer monitors its view of the mempool and runs a re-implementation of Bitcoin Cash Node's transaction selection algorithm to determine the transactions it expects to see in upcoming blocks (source code here). Since there is a continual flow of new transactions, this algorithm runs every 2 seconds, and as a result, you will see the transactions projected to be in upcoming blocks change in near real-time.

At the moment a new block is mined, BCH Explorer saves a snapshot of its projected block template for the next block. We call this snapshot the expected block for the block height in question, and it serves as the basis for the block audit.

When details for an expected block and actual block are available, we can compare them. The purpose of block audits is to deduce when miners intentionally include or exclude transactions from blocks they mine. Since this information cannot be precisely known, BCH Explorer uses a handful of heuristics to accomplish this.

Block audits highlight transactions in different colors to convey these heuristics:

  • ■Added

    A transaction is highlighted blue if it is not present in the expected block, present in the actual block, and also either:

    • far out of the expected feerate range, meaning the miner may have intentionally prioritized the transaction
    • not in the mempool at all, meaning the miner may have accepted the transaction out-of-band

    Added transactions do not negatively affect block health.

  • ■Recently broadcasted

    A transaction is highlighted dark pink if it is present in the expected block, not present in the actual block, and was first seen by BCH Explorer's Bitcoin Cash node within 3 minutes of the block being mined.

    Due to network latency and other factors, it can take time for a miner's Bitcoin Cash nodes to receive a transaction, so we do not assume a miner has intentionally excluded such a transaction from a block.

    Recently-broadcasted transactions do not negatively affect block health.

  • ■Marginal fee rate

    A transaction is darkened if it is at the low end of the expected feerate range and missing in either the expected block or the actual block. This covers two cases:

    • Marginal fee rate: the transaction has a fee rate so close to the block's cutoff that it may have been displaced by another transaction competing for the last bytes of space.
    • Selected (marginal): the transaction was a borderline candidate in the projected block template, sitting at the boundary of what would be included.

    In either case the deviation is not considered notable, and these transactions do not negatively affect block health.

  • ■Removed / No match

    A transaction is highlighted bright pink under two circumstances:

    • Removed (censored): the transaction is present in the expected block, not present in the actual block, and qualifies as neither recently-broadcasted nor marginal-fee. It has been in the mempool long enough to be widely propagated and has a feerate well within the range expected for the block. There is a chance the miner intentionally excluded it.
    • No match: a transaction in the projected block could not be matched to any transaction in the actual block, and the projected data is considered stale. This shares the same color but does not affect block health.

    Only Removed transactions negatively affect block health.

    Block health is calculated as matches / (matches + removed), where only truly removed transactions count against it.

See how results of the block audit are used to devise the block health score below.

Because of this feature's resource usage and availability requirements, it is only supported on official bchexplorer.cash instance.

Block health is a measure of how many transactions appear to be intentionally excluded from a block—a block without any transactions that appear intentionally excluded will have 100% health, while a block with 1 or more transactions that appear intentionally excluded will have sub-100% health.

How is it calculated? Let sexpected be the set of all transactions in BCH Explorer's expected block and let sactual be the set of all transactions in the actual block. Then let n be the number of all transactions in both sexpected and sactual.

Furthermore, let r be the number of transactions BCH Explorer deduces were intentionally excluded from sactual.

Block health is calculated as n / ( n + r ).

The number of transactions appearing in both sexpected and sactual is used (instead of a block's full transaction count) in order to minimize chances that block health is inadvertently impacted by transactions that were most likely not intentionally excluded:

  • recently-broadcast transactions, since the miner may simply not have received them
  • certain low-feerate transactions, since the miner may have opted to replace them with more profitable out-of-band transactions

As a result, block health is not intended to be a measure of how closely an expected block resembles an actual block. The actual block can be vastly different from the expected block, but if no transactions appear to be intentionally excluded, it will have a high health rating (for example).

A "sigop" is a way of accounting for the cost of "signature operations" in Bitcoin Cash script, like OP_CHECKSIG, OP_CHECKSIGVERIFY, OP_CHECKMULTISIG and OP_CHECKMULTISIGVERIFY

These signature operations incur different costs depending on whether they are single or multi-sig operations, and on where they appear in a Bitcoin Cash transaction.

Since May 2020 Bitcoin Cash uses SigChecks that replaces the SigOps limit. With a 32MB blocksize that would be 226,950 SigChecks per block. And the per transation limit is 3000 SigChecks.

The projected mempool blocks represent what we expect the next blocks would look like if they were mined right now, and so each projected block follows all of the same rules and constraints as real mined blocks.

Those constraints can sometimes cause transactions with lower feerates to be included "ahead" of transactions with higher rates.

For example, if one projected block has a very small amount of space left, it might be able to fit one more tiny low feerate transaction, while larger higher feerate transactions have to wait for the following block.

A similar effect can occur when there are a large number of transactions with very many sigops. In that scenario, each projected block can only include up to 80,000 sigops worth of transactions, after which the remaining space can only be filled by potentially much lower fee transactions with zero sigops.

In extreme cases this can produce several projected blocks in a row with overlapping fee ranges, as a result of each projected block containing both high-feerate high-sigop transactions and lower feerate zero-sigop transactions.
